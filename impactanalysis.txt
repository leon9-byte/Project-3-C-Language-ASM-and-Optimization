As it became more optimized from O0 -> O1, I expected there to be less lines. What was really interesting through was form O1 -> O3, the number of lines of assembly actually increased when it was more optimized.

O0->O1 was 210 lines -> 180 lines
O1->O3 was 180 lines -> 188 lines

At the O0 level, it does not do any analysis. It is simply a direct translation to assembly and everything just lives on the stack. 
The offset, n, loop counters i, and the FILE* pointer all live in named stack slots and are read/written on every use.
This generates many redundant load/store pairs that a register allocator would eliminate.
	.file	"optimization.c"
	.text
	.section	.rodata (STRING LITERALS ARE STORED IN .rodata)
.LC0:
	.string	"Usage: %s <filename>\n"
.LC1:
	.string	"rb"
.LC2:
	.string	"Unable to open file: %s\n"
	.align 8  (NOT THERE IN SUBSEQUENT OPTIMIZATIONS)
.LC3: (OMITTED LATER)
	.string	"File opened. First fread test...\n"
.LC4: (SAME HERE)
	.string	"Read %zu bytes\n"
.LC5:
	.string	"%08lx "
.LC6:
	.string	"%02x "
.LC7:
	.string	"   "
.LC8:
	.string	"|"
	.text
	.globl	main
	.type	main, @function



O1 actually includes the optimizations where it no longer just lives on the stack but instead changes the way the data is structured and located. 
	.file	"optimization.c"

.LC0:
[ETC ETC ETC]
.LC6:
	.string	"|"
	.text
printf gets replaced with __fprintf_chk@PLT
Variables are now in registers
%r15, %r14, %r13, %r12, %rbp, %rbx

O3 is the most aggressive optimization
.LC6:
	.string	"|"

	.p2align 4 (NOT PRESENT IN OTHER 2)

Its string section is also .rodata.str1.1,"aMS",@progbits,1 and uses the same __printf_chk as O1
There were also 6 instances of xorl in O3 compared to 1 instance of xorl in O0 and O1.
